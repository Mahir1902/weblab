# WebLab — Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
Small business owners in Australia (tradies, local services, restaurants, retail) who are not technical. They're time-poor, skeptical of tech jargon, and have probably been burned by cheap freelancers or generic template agencies before. They visit the site on their phone, often during a quick break. They don't read — they scan. They make decisions on gut feel and social proof, not feature lists.

**Job to be done:** Quickly decide whether WebLab is worth a conversation. They need to feel: "this team is serious, they've done this before, and they won't waste my money."

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** Trusted · Sharp · Urgent

- **Trusted**: Feels established, credible, like a senior craftsperson not a hustle account
- **Sharp**: Precise language, clean execution — signals that the work will be the same
- **Urgent**: Creates a quiet pull — makes the visitor feel like not booking a call is a missed opportunity

Emotional goal: "I need to work with them." Not impressed. Not reassured. *Wanting in.*

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme:** Dark. Justified — small business owners browsing at night or on a worksite in variable lighting. The dark theme also signals premium without explanation.

**Visual tone:** Refined authority. Not try-hard. Not corporate-cold. The aesthetic equivalent of a high-end tradesperson who shows up on time, does immaculate work, and quotes you a price that makes you wince but you pay it anyway because you know it's right.

**Color:** Deep near-black background (#0A0A0A), Electric Blue accent (#3B82F6). Neutrals should be warm-tinted toward blue — not pure gray. Accents should feel rare and intentional.

**Typography:** Headings should feel substantial and confident — a display face with weight and character, not just "a sans-serif." Body copy should be highly readable for non-technical readers — comfortable line-height, generous spacing, nothing that feels dense.

**Anti-references:**
- Generic dark-blue agency template sites (Webflow showcase bro-agency aesthetic)
- Discount / freelancer vibes — low contrast, cramped spacing, stock photo heroes
- Gimmick-heavy sites — parallax for its own sake, particle effects, cursor effects

**References to draw from (feel, not copy):**
- High-end trade services (architects, specialist engineers) — confident, uncluttered
- Premium SaaS landing pages that are dark but *warm* — purposeful color use
- The aesthetic of "we don't need to try hard because the work speaks"

### Design Principles

1. **Premium legibility over visual complexity.** Non-technical small business owners should never have to decode the layout. Hierarchy must be immediately obvious. Every heading should work as a standalone statement.

2. **Precision signals trust.** Meticulous spacing, consistent alignment, and intentional details tell a non-technical visitor "this team is careful." Sloppy margins and inconsistent padding erode confidence faster than any copy.

3. **Restraint creates desire.** Don't explain everything. Don't animate everything. Leave breathing room — what's *not* there is as important as what is. The site should feel like it doesn't need to impress you; that's what makes it impressive.

4. **Every element earns its place.** No decorative dividers, no "hero metric" grids with big numbers, no animations that don't convey meaning. If removing an element wouldn't be missed, remove it.

5. **Urgency through exclusivity, not pressure.** Don't use countdown timers or scarcity language. Create urgency by making the brand feel like something you don't want to miss out on — not something being sold at you.
